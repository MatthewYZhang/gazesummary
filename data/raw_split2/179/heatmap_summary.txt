The invention of the printing press in the mid-15th century by Johannes Gutenberg initiated a new era of information dissemination and significantly impacted European society. Prior to this, books were painstakingly copied by hand, mainly by monks, leading to their scarcity and expense. The printing press mechanized this process, enabling mass production, triggering a cascade of changes. It led to a dramatic increase in book availability and affordability, extending access beyond the elite to merchants and artisans. This fueled literacy and knowledge spread beyond traditional institutions. The printing press played a crucial role in the Renaissance, sparking intellectual debates through the widespread availability of ancient texts. Humanist scholars used the printing press to promote their ideas. The Reformation was significantly influenced by the printing press. The printing press allowed reformers to bypass the Church. Scientists like Nicolaus Copernicus and Galileo Galilei published their findings. The printing press also presented challenges to authorities. The printing press transformed European communication and culture.
