The resurgence of populist movements has become prominent globally. Economic anxieties, fueled by globalization, automation, and financialization, have led to increased income inequality and job displacement. Populist leaders often exploit these anxieties by scapegoating immigrants, minorities, or foreign competitors and promising to protect domestic industries, create jobs, and redistribute wealth. This resonates with ordinary citizens feeling left behind.

Moreover, scholars are debating the causes and consequences of this phenomenon, with populist leaders often challenging established political norms and institutions. The article also considers that the movement's critics warn of undermined democratic norms and institutions and a threat to democratic resilience. The rise of populism poses significant risks to democratic norms and institutions. Understanding the causes of populism is crucial for ensuring the resilience of democracy in the 21st century.
